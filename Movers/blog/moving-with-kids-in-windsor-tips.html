Moving With Kids in Windsor:
Tips for a Smoother Transition

Moving with children adds a layer of emotional and logistical complexity to an already demanding process. Kids thrive on routine and can find a move disorienting — but with the right preparation, you can keep them comfortable, involved, and even excited about the change.

Tell Them Early and Honestly

Children handle transitions better when they're not surprised by them. Tell your kids about the move as early as possible, in age-appropriate terms:

  • Toddlers and preschoolers (2–5): Keep it simple and positive — "We're moving to a new home! Your toys and bed are coming with us."
  • School-age children (6–12): Give more context — explain the new neighbourhood, their new school, what stays the same
  • Teenagers: Be honest about the reasons for the move and give them space to process their feelings — dismissing their concerns makes the transition harder

💡 Let children visit the new home before moving day if possible — familiarity reduces anxiety significantly, especially for younger kids.

Involve Kids in the Process

  • Let them pack their own special box of toys and items that will travel with them (not in the truck)
  • Give older children a job on moving day — keeping track of boxes, managing the pet, being a "helper"
  • Let them choose something about their new room — a paint colour, a poster wall, where their bed goes

Keeping Kids Safe on Moving Day

Moving day with young children in the house is a safety concern. The best approach:

  • Arrange for a trusted adult to take children off-site for the busiest part of the day
  • If kids must be present, designate one "safe room" that isn't being touched by movers
  • Keep pets secured in a separate room or off-site as well — a loose dog on moving day is a liability
  • Put up baby gates near active loading areas to prevent young children from wandering near the truck

Setting Up the New Home for Kids First

When unloading at the new home, prioritize setting up the children's bedrooms first. A familiar bed, familiar toys, and a recognizable space in an unfamiliar house gives kids an anchor and helps them settle much faster.

After the Move: Supporting the Transition

  • Maintain regular routines (meal times, bedtime) as quickly as possible
  • Explore the new neighbourhood together — find the nearest park, playground, or ice cream shop
  • Connect them with new activities — sports teams, clubs, or community programs — to help them build friendships
