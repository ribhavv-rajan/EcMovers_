How to Prepare a Piano
Before the Movers Arrive

Professional piano movers bring the equipment and expertise — but there are things you need to do before they arrive to ensure the move goes smoothly. Preparation is especially important for pianos because the access route is everything: a crew of four people cannot improvise their way through a tight stairwell they weren't warned about.

Measure the Access Route — Every Step of It

Before moving day, walk the full route from the piano's current position to the exit, noting:

  • Every doorway width — the narrowest point is the critical one
  • Every hallway width and any turns
  • Stairwell width and ceiling height at any landing turns
  • Whether doors open inward or outward and whether they can be removed from hinges
  • Distance from the exit to where the truck will park

Share these measurements with your mover when you book — don't wait for moving day.

Clear the Piano's Surroundings

  • Remove any items sitting on or around the piano (photos, lamps, music books, vases)
  • Move nearby furniture out of the pathway entirely
  • Roll up any rugs along the route — dollies and boards don't move easily over soft surfaces
  • Remove any decorative items from hallways and rooms along the path

💡 A clear, obstacle-free path saves significant time and reduces the risk of accidental damage to your walls, floors, and the piano itself.

Secure the Keyboard Lid

  • For upright pianos: lock the keyboard lid if your piano has a key lock
  • If there's no lock, tape the lid gently with painter's tape — never use strong adhesive tape directly on the finish
  • For grand pianos: the mover will handle the lid removal as part of their process

Protect the Floors

Piano movers use boards and dollies that can leave marks on hardwood or tile. You can help by:

  • Placing a moving blanket or cardboard under the piano legs before the crew arrives (they'll remove it when placing the piano board)
  • Letting the crew know which floors are particularly delicate
  • Moving floor rugs out of the way yourself — the crew shouldn't have to work around them

Be Available to Communicate

Stay close on moving day to answer questions about the piano and the route. If you've identified a potential tight spot, show it to the lead mover before they start — don't wait for them to discover it mid-manoeuvre.
