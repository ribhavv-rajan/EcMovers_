How to Move Heavy Furniture
Through Stairs and Tight Spaces

Stairs, narrow hallways, tight stairwell landings, and low ceilings are the most common causes of damage — and delays — during home moves in Windsor. Knowing what to measure, what to disassemble, and what the professionals bring to handle difficult spaces makes a significant difference.

Measure Before Moving Day — Not During

The single most preventable problem in furniture moving is discovering on moving day that something doesn't fit. Before any large item is moved:

  • Measure the item at its widest point in all three dimensions
  • Measure every doorway it needs to pass through (width and height)
  • Measure stairwell width and ceiling height at the turn
  • Check for any light fixtures, railings, or protrusions that reduce usable clearance

💡 A sofa that's 2 cm wider than a stairwell can sometimes still be navigated by tilting — but this needs to be planned before the crew arrives, not improvised on the day.

Disassembly: Often the Right Answer

Many large pieces of furniture that seem impossible to move through tight spaces can be fully or partially disassembled:

  • Bed frames — always disassemble; mattresses travel separately
  • Large wardrobes and armoires — remove doors and drawers first
  • Sectional sofas — most sections separate; know where the connectors are
  • Dining tables — most legs unscrew or unbolt
  • Bookcases and shelving units — remove shelves before moving

What Professional Movers Use for Stairs

  • Furniture dollies — for flat-surface movement before and after stairs
  • Moving straps and harnesses — allow two people to carry items on stairs without gripping awkwardly
  • Stair-climbing dollies — for very heavy items like large appliances or pianos
  • Furniture sliders — for repositioning heavy items on hard floors without lifting

The "Piano Pivot" Technique

For sofas and large items that can't quite make a corner, professional movers use a pivot technique — standing the piece on end or tilting it diagonally to navigate the turn. This requires knowing the exact clearances and having enough people to control the piece throughout the rotation.
